Go back in time to mid-1980's Santa Monica, California.

My colleagues and I were very involved in accounting/inventory control software for a variety of businesses. At around the same time IBM PC became more prevalant and Apple just revealed the Macintosh. User memory partitioning on our Alpa Micro mini-frames was usually around 32K RAM each, so as software became more sophisticated, one started to bump into that upper limitation. Adding RAM was not only expensive but also limited due to the finite number of bus slots available. Although a primitive form of memory paging could be implemented, it was painfully slow giving the access speed at the time. Typical user access was with a "dumb" terminal, such as the VT100 (from Digital Equipment Corporation or DEC) pictured on this page. So the idea was born to write drivers to replace these terminals with PC and Macintosh equipment and use them as a smart terminal that could use local processing capacity to augment the throughput and enhance the usage in several other ways. After many weeks of Western Digital WD16 and Motorola 68000 assembly programming (Yes! Those were the days) we had good working drivers that could be installed on the Alpa Micro. We enjoyed a brief period of success, but alas, the mini-frame trchnology was doomed and replaced by network and other more advanced technologies.
